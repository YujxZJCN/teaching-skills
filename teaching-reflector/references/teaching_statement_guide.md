# Teaching Statement Guide

Genre knowledge for `statement_writer_agent` (and `portfolio_builder_agent`, since the
two artifacts must cohere). The teaching statement is the most boilerplate-prone genre
in academia: committees read dozens, and the interchangeable ones blur together. The
only durable defense is specificity — real practices, real evidence, real voice.

## 1. Genre norms by purpose

| Purpose | What the readers are asking | Calibration |
|---------|----------------------------|-------------|
| R1 tenure/promotion | "Is teaching competent and improving, without consuming the research agenda?" | Efficient, evidence-forward; improvement trajectory (iteration_history shines); 1–1.5 pages; SoTL output is a differentiator |
| Teaching-focused college | "Is teaching the center of this person's professional identity?" | Deeper pedagogical reasoning expected; student-development focus; 2 pages acceptable; thin statements are disqualifying here |
| Job market | "Can they teach *our* students *our* courses, starting fall?" | Forward-looking; name course types from the posting; flexibility and readiness over polished philosophy; 1–2 pages per posting norms |
| Teaching award | "What is distinctive — why this person among strong nominees?" | One sharp narrative thread beats comprehensive coverage; lean hardest on concrete evidence and student outcomes |

Institution- and field-specific expectations vary (some units issue prompts or rubrics):
`[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT: any stated requirements from the posting/committee]` before
drafting to a purpose.

## 2. The elicitation question set

Phase 1 of `statement_writer_agent` — adapt the arc, don't march through it. Probe every
generic answer down to an observable practice.

1. **What do you actually do in class that you'd defend?** Specific moves, specific
   assignments. Follow-up: "Walk me through last Tuesday's class."
2. **What changed in your teaching, and why?** What did you stop doing? What triggered
   it — evidence, a failure, a colleague? (Pull passport `iteration_history` if present.)
3. **What do students say, in their words?** Real quotes from evals/emails — route to
   `eval_analysis_report.md` exemplar quotes if a run exists.
4. **Which course are you proudest of, and what does that reveal?** The pride locates
   the philosophy more reliably than asking for the philosophy.
5. **What's hardest about learning your subject, and what do you do about it?** Connects
   discipline expertise to teaching choices — the intersection committees value most.
6. **What would a student who got the most out of your course say they can now do?**
   (The outcomes lens, §1–2, applied to identity.)
7. **What do you not believe about teaching?** A genuine dissent from a fashionable
   practice, with reasoning, is often the most memorable paragraph available.

## 3. Structure patterns that work

No mandatory outline — but the load-bearing unit is the **claim → practice → evidence
loop**:

> *Claim:* one belief, stated plainly. → *Practice:* what the professor concretely does
> because of it (named assignment, named move, named course). → *Evidence:* what
> happened — coded eval themes with counts, item-level performance, a peer
> observation, an iteration_history entry.

A working statement is 3–4 such loops plus a short opening (a concrete scene or problem
beats a credo) and a short close (trajectory: what they're working on next — committees
read "still improving" as strength, not weakness). Common workable arrangements:

- **Problem-first:** the central difficulty of teaching this discipline → how their
  practice answers it (strong for discipline identity)
- **Evolution:** early approach → evidence that changed it → current practice (strong
  when iteration_history is rich)
- **Three commitments:** 3 loops under plain-language headers (safe, clear; risks
  listiness — needs a connecting thread)

Every claim must also be evidenced in the portfolio when one exists — statement claims
are a subset of portfolio-evidenced claims (the alignment principle, §2, applied to
careers). Orphan claims get flagged, then evidenced or cut.

## 4. Cliché and red-flag table

Checked against every draft before it's shown to the professor.

| Phrase / move | Why it fails | Instead |
|---------------|--------------|---------|
| "I am passionate about teaching" | Asserted by everyone; evidence of nothing | Show a practice only a person who cares would sustain — let the reader conclude it |
| "Students are not empty vessels" / "sage on the stage vs guide on the side" | Decades-stale strawman; signals secondhand philosophy | State what you *do* believe and the practice it produces |
| "I foster critical thinking" | Unfalsifiable and universal | Name the assignment that demands it and what students produce |
| "I create a safe / inclusive environment" (unsupported) | Claim every statement makes; meaningless without mechanism | The specific practice: structured turn-taking, anonymous polling, the accessible-materials workflow — and why |
| Jargon bingo ("constructivist scaffolded praxis", "synergistic pedagogies") | Reads as performance for an imagined audience; obscures practice | Plain language; one technical term only where it earns its keep and is shown in action |
| "Technology-enhanced learning" as a virtue in itself | Tool-first thinking; committees have seen LMS-feature lists | Lead with the learning problem; the tool appears as the solution if at all |
| An anecdote with no source ("one student told me…") that the professor can't place | If unverifiable by the professor it cannot ship — fabrication risk is career-level (Iron Rule 4) | Use only quotes/events the professor can locate; otherwise cut |
| Quoting eval averages as proof of excellence ("my 4.8/5.0 shows…") | Evidence-illiterate per §11; alert committees discount it | Coded comment themes with counts, triangulated evidence — and the statement reads as more credible, not less |
| Teaching-as-performance framing ("captivate", "entertain", "my lectures are theater") | Centers the instructor; the genre's readers look for student learning | Recenter on what *students do* in the session and what changes for them |
| Universal coverage (every course, award, and committee mentioned) | A statement isn't a CV; dilution kills memorability | 3–4 loops max; the CV and portfolio carry the rest |

## 5. Length and format conventions

- **1–2 pages**, single-spaced, normal margins; toward 1 for R1 and job-market packets,
  toward 2 for teaching-focused institutions. Over 2 pages reads as inability to
  prioritize.
- First person, plain prose, no epigraphs, no section numbering; short plain headers
  acceptable in 2-page statements.
- Citations: at most 1–2, only where a specific practice genuinely derives from a
  source. A reference list on a teaching statement is a genre error.
- Match any stated formatting requirements from the committee
  (`[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT]`).

## 6. Coherence with the portfolio

When statement and portfolio travel together (tenure, awards), they are one argument in
two registers: the statement makes claims; the portfolio holds the evidence. The joint
audit before either ships:

1. Every statement claim → a portfolio artifact (cut or evidence the orphans)
2. Every major portfolio artifact → connected to some narrative thread (or it's dead
   weight)
3. Quotes/numbers appearing in both → identical, verbatim
4. Voice consistent across both narratives — committees notice when the statement and
   the portfolio narrative read as different authors

Run the audit again after any late edit to either document.
