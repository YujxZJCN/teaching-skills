# Research Essay — The Atlantic World

**HIST 247 The Atlantic World, 1500–1800** · Worth 35% of your final grade
**Assigned:** W7 (launch + library session) · **Final due:** W15 · **Work mode:** individual

> Demonstration artifact — synthetic course, no real students. This is the A4 project brief,
> built from the suite's TILT project-brief template (Purpose / Task / Criteria).

## Purpose — why this essay exists

This essay is where you show that you can:

1. Pose your own historical question about the Atlantic world and make it researchable.
2. Build an argument from primary sources, reading each as evidence — establishing what it
   is, who made it, and why — rather than as illustration.
3. Distinguish your claim, evidence, and reasoning from summary, and situate your argument
   inside an actual scholarly debate.

History is not a body of facts to be recited; it is an ongoing argument about what the
evidence will and will not support. The capability this essay builds — turning a curiosity
into a question, a question into an argument, and an argument into prose that other people
can check — is the core of historical work, in graduate study, in archives and museums, in
law, and in any field that runs on evidence. The staged design exists because that is how
the work is actually done: nobody writes a good research essay in one pass.

## Task — what you'll do

You will write an **original research essay of roughly 3,000–4,000 words** that poses a
historical question about some aspect of the Atlantic world (1500–1800), argues a thesis in
answer to it, and supports that thesis with analysis of **primary sources you locate
yourself** (from the digitized collections introduced in the week-7 library session or
others you clear with the instructor) read against the relevant secondary literature.

The topic is yours, within the period and field — that personalization is deliberate: your
question, your sources, your argument. You clear the topic at the proposal stage so no one
heads down an unworkable path.

### Milestones

| Milestone | Due | What you submit | Weight / check | Feedback you'll get |
|-----------|-----|-----------------|----------------|---------------------|
| M1 — Proposal | W8 | ~600 words: your question, why it matters, 3–5 primary sources you have located, and 2–3 secondary works you will engage | 7% | Written comments within 7 days: is the question researchable, are the sources adequate |
| M2 — Reviewed draft | W12 | A full draft (≥ 2,500 words) **plus a 1-paragraph revision memo** on what you changed since the proposal and why | 8% | Written comments + a brief conference in/after the W12 workshop |
| M3 — Final essay | W15 | The finished essay (3,000–4,000 words) **plus a 1-paragraph revision memo** on what you changed in response to the draft feedback | 20% | Final grade with rubric and comments |

Milestones are not bureaucracy: the staged trail is how strong work gets built, and the
revision memos are part of what is assessed — they make your *thinking between drafts*
visible. A milestone that arrives as a fresh attempt with no trace of the prior feedback
has missed the point of the design.

## Criteria — what good work looks like

Excellent work in this essay:

- **Poses a genuine, answerable question** and states a thesis that takes a position (LO5) —
  not "this paper will discuss…" but a claim someone could disagree with.
- **Argues from primary sources read as evidence** (LO5, LO4): each source is contextualized
  — provenance, audience, purpose — and made to do argumentative work, not decoration.
- **Engages the historiography** (LO3): locates the argument within a real scholarly debate
  and is honest about what other historians have claimed.
- **Resists presentism and weighs the limits of the sources** (LO4): reconstructs the past on
  its own terms and acknowledges what the evidence cannot show.
- **Shows its revision** (LO5): the memos and the trajectory from proposal to final
  demonstrate a question and an argument that genuinely developed.

Full rubric: distributed with the brief (analytic rubric, criteria mirror the bullets above —
no hidden criteria). Anchor examples of past strong essays are discussed in the week-7
launch where the instructor provides them; none are fabricated here.

## AI use on this project

> **Tier D — permitted with disclosure.** The staged milestones and the revision memos make
> your own historical thinking visible regardless of what tools you used, so tools are
> allowed — but the *analysis of sources and the argument must be yours*, which is exactly
> what the proposal-to-final trajectory is built to verify.
>
> If you use AI tools, append a short disclosure at each milestone: which tool, what you
> asked it, and what you changed. Disclosure is never penalized; non-disclosure where
> required is an integrity matter. Note that generative tools routinely invent plausible-
> looking sources and citations — every primary and secondary source in your essay must be
> one you have personally located and verified.

## FAQ

- **Can I write on a topic outside the period?** No — stay within 1500–1800 and the Atlantic
  frame; clear edge cases at the proposal.
- **Can I reuse a topic from a reading response or the primary-source paper?** You may build
  on a source or question you met earlier, but the essay must go substantially beyond it.
- **What if my question turns out to be unworkable?** That is what the proposal stage is
  for; the conference after M1 exists to redirect you early.
- **What happens if a milestone slips?** Late days apply per the syllabus; but milestones
  feed each other, so a late proposal compresses the time you have to use its feedback.
