Policies
Grading schemeFinal grade = weighted sum per the assessment plan. Reading responses (A2) use a lightly specs-flavored scheme: each response is marked pass / revise against a posted spec (a defensible claim, at least one source read as evidence not illustration, and contextualization of that source); a "revise" may be resubmitted once within one week and, if it then meets the spec, counts as a pass. The best 5 of 6 passes count. All other assessments are graded on their rubrics. No curve. Letter cutoffs: A >= 93, A- 90-92, B+ 87-89, B 83-86, B- 80-82, C+ 77-79, C 70-76, D 60-69, F < 60.
Late workResearch-essay milestones (A4) and the primary-source paper (A3): 3 free late days pooled across the term (max 2 on one artifact); afterwards -1/3 letter grade per day, up to 3 days, then not accepted. Reading responses (A2): no make-ups — best-5-of-6 absorbs absences, and the revise option absorbs a weak attempt. The take-home synthesis (A5) has a fixed submission window and is not eligible for late days.
AI usePer-assessment tiers (shared/ai_era_integrity.md). Tier P: A1 seminar participation and discussion leadership — live, in-person, unaided by construction. Tier D: A2 reading responses, A3 primary-source paper, A4 research essay — generative AI permitted as a tool with a short disclosure (which tool, what you asked, what you changed); disclosure is never penalized, non-disclosure where required is an integrity matter. Tier O: A5 take-home synthesis essay — AI collaboration is expected; you are graded on judgment over the tool's output (verification, selection, and what you add beyond it), with a required process note. Full table with rationales in syllabus.md.

Learner profile
Prior knowledgeHistory and area-studies majors who have completed at least one prior history course. Strong readers with solid general historical literacy. Primary-source analysis experience is mixed — some have done a methods course, others have only read sources as illustration rather than as evidence to be interrogated.
MotivationA topical seminar that most students elect toward the major; many intend graduate study or careers in archives, museums, law, and teaching (professor's report — not surveyed).
Accessibilityunknown

Known difficulties

  • Presentism — judging past actors and institutions by present-day values rather than reconstructing their context
  • Confusing summary with argument — restating what a source says instead of advancing a claim about it
  • Weak source contextualization — reading a document without establishing its provenance, audience, and purpose
