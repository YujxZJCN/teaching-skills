Dear Members of the Admissions Committee,

I write in strong support of A. Student's application to your MA program in History. I taught
A. Student in HIST 247, *The Atlantic World, 1500–1800* — a 15-week discussion seminar of 18
students — and worked with her in office hours through her research essay. I have known her
for only that one term, but it is a small, writing-intensive seminar in which I read three
substantial pieces of her work and watched her argue weekly, so I can speak to her historical
ability with confidence even on a short acquaintance [C1].

What distinguishes A. Student is that she reads primary sources as evidence to be
interrogated rather than as illustration. Her primary-source analysis paper examined a 1789
plantation inventory, and it was the clearest paper in the seminar at doing the unglamorous,
essential work first — establishing what the document actually was, who compiled it, and why,
before drawing a single inference from it [C2]. This is the habit that separates students who
will do well in a graduate program from those who will struggle, and she already has it.

She is also a genuine contributor to a room. When she co-led our seminar on the Haitian
Revolution, she arrived not with a summary of the readings but with three questions that
reframed them; the discussion that day was the strongest of the term, a judgment several of
her peers volunteered as well [C3]. Over the term I watched a related skill develop in her
written work: her early reading responses tended toward summary, but by mid-term she was
reliably isolating each historian's actual claim and warrant and placing it within a debate —
a visible, self-driven trajectory across the six responses [C4]. And in a seminar on this
period, where it is easy to substitute present-day judgment for historical reconstruction,
she was notably disciplined: in our week on memory and legacy she separated moral evaluation
from the work of reconstructing how past actors understood their own world, and pressed her
peers to do the same without lecturing them [C5].

Her research essay is the clearest evidence of her readiness for graduate work. She posed her
own question, located a set of digitized merchant letters I had not pointed her toward, and
built an original — if appropriately modest — argument about credit and kinship in an Atlantic
trading network; the revision from draft to final was substantial and self-directed [C6]. It
was among the two or three strongest essays in the seminar [C6].

On the strength of this work I would place A. Student in the top three of these eighteen
students for readiness to undertake graduate historical research, and I make that comparison
deliberately [C7]. I should be candid about the limits of what I can attest: I have taught
this seminar once, so I cannot rank her across years or against a longer run of students, and
I have no basis to speak to her language preparation for archival work, which never came up
between us [C1, C7]. Within the scope I do know — her reading, her argument, her conduct in a
seminar — she is excellent, and I recommend her warmly and without reservation for the work
your program asks of its students.

I am glad to answer any questions.

Sincerely,
Prof. EXAMPLE
Department of History, EXAMPLE University
hist247-instructor@example.edu
