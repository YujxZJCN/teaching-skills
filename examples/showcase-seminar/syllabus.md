## What this seminar is about

The Atlantic Ocean did not separate four continents so much as bind them into a single,
violent, generative world. Between roughly 1500 and 1800, European empires, West African
states, and Indigenous American polities were drawn into webs of conquest, forced and free
migration, the slave trade, commodity flows, and exchanges of people, plants, microbes, and
ideas. This seminar studies that world not as a settled story but as a field of argument:
we read primary sources as evidence to be interrogated, and we read historians as people
making claims we can test.

Two habits of mind run through everything we do. First, **contextualization** — a document
means little until you know who made it, for whom, and why. Second, the discipline of
**resisting presentism** — reconstructing how past actors understood their own world before
we judge it by ours. Neither comes naturally; both are skills you build here.

This is a discussion- and writing-intensive course. You are expected to come having read,
ready to argue from the texts, and willing to revise your view when a peer reads a source
better than you did.

## Learning outcomes

By the end of this seminar, you will be able to:

1. **(LO1)** Explain the principal systems, actors, and chronology of the Atlantic world, 1500–1800 — empires, the slave trade and slavery, commodity and migration flows, and exchanges among European, African, and Indigenous American societies
2. **(LO2)** Apply primary-source analysis methods — establishing provenance, audience, purpose, and genre — to interpret an individual document as historical evidence
3. **(LO3)** Analyze a historiographical argument: distinguish an author's claim, evidence, and warrant from mere summary, and locate the argument within a scholarly debate
4. **(LO4)** Evaluate competing historical interpretations on their evidentiary and contextual merits, resisting presentism and weighing the limits of the available sources
5. **(LO5)** Create an original, archivally grounded research essay that poses a historical question, builds an argument from primary sources, and situates it in the secondary literature

## How this seminar works

Each 150-minute meeting is a discussion, not a lecture. A short framing from the instructor
opens the session; the bulk of the time is spent arguing from the week's readings, led in
part by the two students assigned to co-lead that week. Discussion leadership means coming
with questions that open the texts up, not a summary of them. Reading is heavy by design —
roughly 70 pages a week of mixed primary and secondary material — because a seminar runs on
what everyone has read. Out of class, expect about six hours a week: reading and annotating,
short reading responses, and the staged research essay.

The research essay (A4) is staged on purpose: a proposal in week 8, a reviewed draft in
week 12, and a final essay in week 15, each accompanied by a short revision memo describing
what you changed in response to feedback. The staging is not bureaucracy — it is how a real
essay gets built, and the trail of revision is part of what is assessed. The full brief is
in the research-essay project brief.

### Reading responses: a specs-flavored pass/revise scheme

Reading responses are graded against a posted **specification**, not on a point scale. A
response **passes** when it does three things: advances a defensible claim about the week's
reading (not a summary of it), reads at least one source as *evidence* rather than as
illustration, and contextualizes that source (who made it, for whom, why). A response that
misses the spec is marked **revise**; you may resubmit it once within a week, and if it then
meets the spec it counts as a pass. Six responses are assigned and your **best five passes**
count. This scheme exists to move you off summary and toward argument early — and to make
the standard explicit rather than hidden in a grade.

**Grading scale:** weighted sum above; no curve. A ≥ 93, A− 90–92, B+ 87–89, B 83–86,
B− 80–82, C+ 77–79, C 70–76, D 60–69, F < 60.
**Late work:** the primary-source paper and research-essay milestones share 3 free late days
pooled across the term (max 2 on one artifact), then −1/3 letter grade per day up to 3 days.
Reading responses have no make-ups (best-5-of-6 plus the revise option absorb a weak week);
the take-home synthesis has a fixed window and is not eligible for late days.
**Regrades:** written request within 7 days of grade release; the whole item is re-read.
**Feedback:** reading responses — by the next seminar; primary-source paper — within 10 days;
research-essay milestones — within 7 days, because the feedback feeds your next milestone.

## AI use in this course

Different assignments have different rules, each chosen for a reason:

| Assignment | Policy | Why |
|------------|--------|-----|
| Participation & discussion leadership (A1) | **Tier P — no generative AI** | This is a live, in-person conversation about texts you have read; it is unaided by construction, and that is the point. |
| Reading responses (A2) | **Tier D — permitted with disclosure** | Short analytical writing where tools may help you draft or check prose; the spec (claim, evidence, contextualization) tests your own reading, and a one-line disclosure keeps the use honest. |
| Primary-source analysis paper (A3) | **Tier D — permitted with disclosure** | The skill being assessed is *your* interrogation of one document; tools may assist the writing, the analysis must be yours, and you disclose what you used. |
| Research essay (A4) | **Tier D — permitted with disclosure** | The staged milestones and revision memos make your own thinking visible regardless of the tools; disclose tool use at each stage. |
| Take-home synthesis essay (A5) | **Tier O — AI collaboration expected** | Here the assignment is explicitly about working *with* tools well: you are graded on judgment — what you verify, what you select, and what you add beyond the tool's output — and you submit a short process note. |

When AI use is permitted with disclosure (Tier D), append to your submission which tool you
used, what you asked it, and what you changed. Disclosure is never penalized; non-disclosure
where required is an integrity matter. There are no AI-detection tools in this course — they
do not work reliably, and the design above makes them unnecessary.
