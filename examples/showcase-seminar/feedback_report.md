## What you did well

- **Your selection and framing of the document is genuinely promising.** Your opening sentence
  — "This letter shows how a Liverpool merchant managed risk across an ocean he never crossed"
  — is a real claim about the source, not a summary of it, and it gives the paper somewhere to
  go. That is exactly the move the assignment is built to reward.
- **You quote the source closely.** In §3 you pull the phrase "as the trade now stands" and
  read it carefully — that is reading a source as evidence, not decoration.

## What to work on (priority order)

**1. Establish the document before you interpret it (the biggest fixable thing).**
- **In your work:** the paper begins interpreting in ¶1 ("This letter shows…") but never
  establishes the basics first — who exactly the writer and recipient are, what a "factor"
  did, why a merchant would commit this to a letter, or how the letter survived to reach an
  archive. The closest you come is one clause in §2 ("a merchant writing to his agent"), which
  is asserted, not established.
- **Why it matters / where you're going:** this is the core of LO2 — provenance, audience,
  purpose. An interpretation built on an uncharacterized source can't be weighed, because the
  reader can't tell what the source is in a position to show. It's also the single skill
  graduate programs and the research essay (A4) most reward.
- **Next step:** before your next paper, write a short "what is this document" paragraph *first*
  — writer, recipient, genre, occasion, audience, and how it reached us — and only then
  interpret. Try it on the merchant letter and you'll see the §2 assertion turn into evidence.

**2. Separate the claim from the summary in the middle of the paper.**
- **In your work:** §4 and §5 (the paragraphs on cargo and credit) narrate what the letter says
  in sequence — "He then writes that… He also mentions…" — without telling the reader what you
  are arguing from it. The argument from ¶1 disappears here and only returns in the conclusion.
- **Why it matters / where you're going:** this is the summary-vs-argument line (LO2/LO4, and
  the habit the whole seminar is built around). A reader should be able to see your claim
  driving each paragraph, not just the first and last.
- **Next step:** give §4 and §5 a topic sentence each that states *your* point, then use the
  letter's lines as evidence for it. Cut any sentence that only reports what the letter says
  without doing analytical work.

**3. Watch the one presentist phrase.**
- **In your work:** in §6 you write that the merchant was "shockingly indifferent to the human
  cargo." The judgment may be fair, but stated this way it substitutes our reaction for an
  analysis of his frame.
- **Why it matters / where you're going:** LO4 asks you to reconstruct before you judge —
  *then* you can judge with force, on the evidence.
- **Next step:** reconstruct first ("the letter treats enslaved people in the same register as
  other freight, which tells us…"), and let the moral weight land from the reconstruction.

## One thing to carry forward

**Characterize the source before you use it.** If you build one habit from this paper, build
the "what is this document" paragraph at the front — it fixes priority 1 directly and quietly
strengthens everything downstream, because an argument grounded in a well-understood source is
harder to knock over. Carry it straight into the research essay proposal.
