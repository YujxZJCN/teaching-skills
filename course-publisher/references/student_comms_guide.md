# Student Communications Guide

The comms rulebook for every student-facing message and page this skill produces.
Evidence base: students read course email on phones, in seconds, mid-task; whatever the
first two lines don't accomplish doesn't happen. These are working mechanics, not style
preferences — agents apply them by default and flag deviations rather than arguing them.

## Action-first email anatomy

Every message answers three questions in this order:

1. **What do I do?** — the action, first sentence, concrete verb.
2. **By when?** — the deadline, **bolded**, day-of-week + date together
   ("**Friday, March 13, 17:00**" — never a bare "Friday" or bare "3/13", which
   misread differently across formats and time zones).
3. **Why / details?** — context after the action, and only context that changes what
   the student does. Background that exists to make the sender feel thorough gets cut.

If a message has no action, say so in the subject ("No action needed:") — and consider
whether it should be a line in the weekly email instead of a message at all.

### Worked rewrites: bad → good

| # | Before | Diagnosis | After |
|---|--------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | "Dear students, I hope this email finds you well. As you know, we have been covering sorting algorithms over the past several weeks, and as part of the assessment plan described in the syllabus, there will be a quiz. The quiz will take place during Thursday's session and will cover the material from weeks 4 and 5. Please make sure to review accordingly." | Action and date buried in sentence four; throat-clearing opener; "review accordingly" prescribes nothing | "Quiz in class **Thursday, Oct 9** — covers weeks 4–5 (sorting). Best prep: redo problem set 4 without notes. 15 minutes, closed book, counts 5% per the syllabus." |
| 2 | "Important update regarding the course schedule: due to unforeseen circumstances related to facility availability, some adjustments have become necessary to our regular meeting arrangements for the upcoming week." | Says a change exists without saying the change; subject-line vagueness in body form | "Thursday's lecture (Oct 9) moves to **room B204** — same time. Nothing else changes. (Our regular room is being renovated this week.)" |
| 3 | "Reminder: many of you have still not submitted the project proposal which was due last week. It is very important that you submit as soon as possible as this affects your grade. Late penalties are described in the syllabus." | Guilt framing at the whole class; "as soon as possible" is not a deadline; makes compliant students reread anxiously | "If your project proposal isn't in yet: submit by **Wednesday, Oct 8, 17:00** to cap the late penalty at 10% (late policy, syllabus §4). Already submitted? You're done — confirmations went out this morning." |

Pattern across all three: the rewrite is shorter, the deadline is bolded and complete,
the addressee is "you with this task" rather than "the class in general," and every
retained sentence changes what the reader does.

## Subject-line conventions

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Weekly email | `[<code>] Week N — <one due/coming item>` | `[CS 201] Week 6 — quiz Thursday, project proposals back` |
| Deadline/action | `[<code>] <action> by <day, date>` | `[CS 201] Submit proposal by Fri, Oct 10` |
| Change | `[<code>] CHANGE: <what> — <date>` | `[CS 201] CHANGE: Thursday's lecture in B204` |
| Same-week action required | `[<code>] URGENT: <action> — <day>` | `[CS 201] URGENT: bring laptop tomorrow` |
| No action | `[<code>] No action needed: <topic>` | `[CS 201] No action needed: grades posted` |

Rules: course code always first (students take five courses; un-prefixed mail is
unattributable in a notification banner). `URGENT` strictly reserved for same-week
action — used twice for non-urgent items, it stops working for the real one. `CHANGE`
strictly reserved for changes to something previously announced. The subject must
survive truncation at ~40 characters with its meaning intact: front-load.

## Reading level and skimmability

- **Front-load.** The most important fact appears in the subject AND the first line —
  preview text is read more often than body text.
- **Bold the deadline.** One bolding per message class: deadlines. Bold everything and
  nothing is bold.
- **One phone screen.** A weekly email longer than ~150 words of body needs a reason;
  an announcement longer than ~80 almost never has one. Cut from the bottom.
- Short sentences, concrete nouns, no nested clauses. Lists over paragraphs whenever
  three or more parallel items appear. Aim at a reader for whom the language of
  instruction is a second language — that reader is in every class.
- One link per purpose, with link text that says where it goes ("Project brief (PDF)",
  never "click here").

## Tone calibration: warm-plain

The target register is **warm-plain**: a competent person who is on the student's side
and respects their time. Not bureaucratic ("Please be advised that students are
expected to…"), not chummy ("Hey gang!! 🎉"), not motivational-poster.

How to sound like the professor and not like AI:

- **Calibrate from samples.** When prior emails or announcements exist, match their
  greeting, sign-off, contraction use, and sentence length. The professor's voice is a
  fact to compile from, like a passport field.
- **Ban list** (these read as AI-generic or bureaucratic filler; never generate them):
  "I hope this email finds you well" · "exciting" / "I'm excited to…" · "delve" ·
  "kindly" · "Please be advised" · "Don't hesitate to reach out" · "As per my previous
  email" · "journey" (about coursework). If the *professor's own samples* use one,
  their voice wins — the ban is on generating these unprompted.
- Warmth is specific, not adjectival: "the proposals were genuinely fun to read" beats
  "great job everyone!"; an honest "I got the date wrong in Tuesday's slides" beats any
  amount of polish.

## Frequency discipline

- **Weekly rhythm beats ad-hoc bursts.** One predictable weekly email, same day each
  week, is read; five scattered messages are not. The weekly email is the default
  carrier — a separate announcement must justify itself by urgency.
- **Consolidation rule:** two or more non-urgent items pending → fold them into the
  next weekly email rather than sending separately. The comms_planner proposes this
  automatically.
- **Repeat rule for changes:** a change is announced once on its own (ASAP) and echoed
  once in the next weekly email, marked as a repeat. More than two tellings trains
  students to ignore the channel; fewer guarantees someone misses it.
- Silence is also a signal: a week with nothing due still gets its weekly email, two
  lines long ("Nothing due this week. Next up: …") — the rhythm is the product.

## Bad-news patterns

Cancellations, postponements, errors in published materials, lost grading time:

1. **Lead with the change and its date.** "Thursday's class (Oct 9) is cancelled" is
   the whole first sentence. Never make students read toward bad news.
2. **State what it means for them** — the part they actually need: what moves, what
   doesn't, what they should do differently.
3. **Apology brief and honest.** One sentence, owning the thing actually owed ("I
   misjudged the grading time — sorry to keep you waiting"). No self-flagellation, no
   blame-shifting passive voice ("mistakes were made"), no compensatory exclamation
   points.
4. **Never spin.** A cancelled class is not "a great opportunity for self-directed
   review." Students know; pretending otherwise spends credibility the next
   announcement needs.

## Multilingual and bilingual notes

- Bilingual rendering on request: full parallel text, action-first structure preserved
  in both languages — never machine-flavored half-translation of selected sentences.
- Terminology follows the bilingual-courseware glossary when one exists in the
  workspace; otherwise keep each term's rendering consistent across the whole term's
  communications and note unilateral choices for the professor.
- Dates and times are the highest-stakes translation surface: always day-of-week +
  date + 24-hour time in both renderings; ordinal/month-order conventions differ and
  "10/9" is two different days in two audiences.
- Names of campus offices, buildings, and systems stay in the institutional language
  (with a gloss if helpful) — students need the searchable string, not a translation.

## Accessibility — email and web (Quality Gate U1)

- **Semantic structure:** real headings in web output (one `h1`, no skipped levels);
  in email, short paragraphs and lists rather than visual formatting tricks.
- **Alt text** for every image; an image of text (a photographed whiteboard, a
  screenshot of a schedule) always has its content in the body or a linked plain
  version.
- **Link text** describes the destination; bare URLs and "here" fail screen readers
  and previews alike.
- **Tables:** header rows always; in email, prefer a list to a wide table — phone
  clients linearize tables unpredictably. On the web, the schedule table needs a
  readable phone linearization (the site builder checks this).
- **Color and emphasis:** never the sole carrier of meaning ("items in red are
  mandatory" fails); pair with words. Bold for deadlines, sparingly, as above.
- Plain text must survive: every message should make complete sense with all
  formatting stripped, because for some recipients it will be.
