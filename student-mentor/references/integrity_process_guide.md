# Academic-Integrity Process Guide

Working reference for `integrity_case_agent`. Governing rule: **this skill never renders
a verdict, judges guilt, estimates a probability of cheating, or recommends a sanction.**
It organizes evidence factually, routes the professor to *their institution's* process,
and drafts neutral, procedurally-correct documentation. The presumption is good faith;
the student has a right to respond; the institution adjudicates.

This guide describes only the **generic shape** of a fair process. Every institution's
actual procedure differs and is binding — so each institution-specific step below is
flagged `[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT: ...]`. Do not invent procedure; route to the office.

## The generic shape of a fair process (route, don't enact)

A defensible academic-integrity process generally moves through these stages. The skill
helps with the documentation around them; it does not replace any of them.

1. **Notice / intake.** The concern is raised through the institution's defined channel.
   Many institutions require the academic-integrity / honor office be notified **before**
   the professor confronts the student — a professor acting alone can void the case.
   `[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT: the office, its intake route, and whether it must be looped in
   first]`
2. **Evidence.** The objective record is assembled factually (see
   `templates/integrity_case_template.md`): observations about the artifacts, what was
   expected, what was found, with exact locations. Observations, never conclusions.
3. **Student response.** The student is informed and given a genuine opportunity to
   explain — in private, within the process, before any finding. This is a right, not a
   courtesy. `[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT: required form, notice period, and the student's
   appeal rights at your institution]`
4. **Institutional adjudication.** A finding (if any) and any sanction are determined
   through the institution's process — committee, dean, honor council, or whatever your
   policy defines. **This is where guilt and consequences are decided — not by this skill,
   and often not unilaterally by the professor.** `[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT: who adjudicates,
   what the professor may decide alone vs. what the office decides, and the deadlines]`

The skill's role spans stage 2 (factual record) and the *neutral drafting* around stages
1 and 3 (notification language, the meeting invitation). It never reaches into stage 4.

## What the professor must look up — never invented here

| Institution-specific fact | Why it cannot be guessed |
|---------------------------|--------------------------|
| Whether the office must be notified before meeting the student | Acting alone can void the case; the order is binding and varies |
| What the professor may decide unilaterally (e.g., a grade penalty) vs. what the office adjudicates | Authority boundaries differ; overstepping is itself a procedural fault |
| Deadlines, required forms, notice periods | Missing a deadline can forfeit the case or the student's rights |
| The student's appeal rights | A process without a real appeal is not defensible |
| The records/privacy channel the matter must stay within | Privacy law differs by jurisdiction — flag generically, never advise on the law |

Every one of these is `[NEEDS PROFESSOR INPUT]` in the artifacts.

## The do-not-do list

1. **No AI-detection tools as evidence.** Per `shared/ai_era_integrity.md` §1, detectors
   produce false positives at rates that make individual accusations indefensible and are
   biased against non-native English writers. A detector score is **not** evidence, must
   never anchor a case, and never enters the evidence record. If the professor has one,
   record that it is unusable and why — then set it aside.
2. **No public accusation.** No class-wide email, no margin comment visible to others, no
   confrontation in front of peers. One private, documented channel only.
3. **No verdict language, ever.** Not in the record, not in the student message, not at
   the checkpoint. "I have questions about your submission" — never "you cheated,"
   "this is plagiarized," "this is AI-generated."
4. **No unilateral sanction** outside the institution's process. The skill never proposes,
   ranks, or estimates a penalty.
5. **No privacy breach.** Keep the matter to the need-to-know channel the process defines;
   do not loop in parents, employers, other students, or other faculty without the
   process's authorization. Generic flag, then route — no legal advice.
6. **No probability, no guilt estimate.** The skill does not total the evidence into a
   likelihood or a confidence. Strength-of-evidence is for the adjudicator, not this tool.

## Tone

Neutral, procedural, and humane. The student may have an innocent explanation; the
documentation must leave room for it. Write every line as if it will be read back during
an appeal — because it may be.
