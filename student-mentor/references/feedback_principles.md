# Feedback Principles (Hattie & Timperley, Operationalized)

Working reference for `feedback_writer_agent`. The evidence base is Pedagogy
Foundations §8; this file turns it into comment-writing mechanics.

## The three questions, operationalized

| Question | In a comment, this looks like | Failure mode it prevents |
|----------|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Where am I going?** (goal) | One line anchoring the comment to the criterion: "The lit review's job here is to motivate your research question." | Comments that correct everything and orient nothing |
| **How am I going?** (status) | The work's position against that goal, citing the work itself: "Sections 1–2 do this well; section 3 catalogs sources without connecting them to the question." | Vague verdicts ("needs work") the student can't locate |
| **Where to next?** (next step) | One concrete, doable action, **stated first or early**: "For the revision, end each section-3 paragraph with one sentence linking the source to your question." | Feedback that diagnoses but doesn't prescribe |

Front-load the next step. Students read the first two lines of a comment and the grade;
bury the actionable part in paragraph three and it functionally doesn't exist.

## Feedback levels: task / process / self-regulation (avoid self)

| Level | Example | Use when |
|-------|---------|----------|
| **Task** | "The null hypothesis in part (b) is stated backwards." | Errors of fact or execution; fastest to act on |
| **Process** | "When a proof stalls like this, check whether the contrapositive is easier — that's the move this problem rewards." | The error pattern will recur; teaches a transferable method |
| **Self-regulation** | "You caught your own unit error in part (a) — run that same check on parts (c) and (d)." | Strong students ready to own their quality control |
| **Self** (avoid) | "You're clearly very smart." | Effectively never — it carries no information about the work and can reduce subsequent risk-taking |

Mix levels deliberately: task for the immediate fix, process for the durable gain.

## Praise that informs vs praise that inflates

- **Informs:** names the move so it can be repeated. "Opening with the counterexample
  before the general claim made the argument easy to follow — keep that structure."
- **Inflates:** evaluates the person or pads the verdict. "Great job overall!" before
  three paragraphs of problems reads as either insincere or confusing.
- Test: delete the praise sentence. If the student loses *information*, it informs.
  If they only lose mood, it inflates — cut it or make it specific.

## Feedback timing economics

Faster + lighter beats slower + exhaustive. Feedback returned after the next assignment
has started is archaeology, not feedback. Practical exchange rates:

- Three sentences within 4 days > a marked-up page within 3 weeks.
- Comment depth budget per submission: one goal line, one status observation with a
  quote, one next step. Everything beyond that is for the *professor's* conscience, not
  the student's learning — spend the saved time on speed.
- Whole-class patterns (the same error in 60% of submissions) are cheaper handled once,
  in class or in an announcement, than 30 times in margins. Flag these to the professor
  rather than duplicating them.

## The comment-bank method (without genericness)

1. Draft the first 5–8 comments individually; patterns will surface.
2. For each recurring pattern, write a **bank entry**: goal line + process-level
   explanation + next step. Leave a mandatory slot: `⟨where it shows up in THIS
   student's work⟩`.
3. Applying an entry **requires filling the slot** with a quote or location from that
   submission. An entry pasted with the slot empty is generic feedback wearing a
   personalized costume — students detect it immediately, and it reads as not-read work.
4. Fairness use: the bank is also the consistency instrument — same pattern, same
   severity, same next step across the batch (same rubric level ⇒ same treatment).
5. Banks persist across assignments and semesters; recurring entries are a signal for
   `teaching-reflector` (a pattern every cohort hits is a course-design issue, not 30
   student issues).

## Worked rewrites: bad → good

| # | Before | Diagnosis | After |
|---|--------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | "Awkward." (margin) | Locates nothing, prescribes nothing | "This sentence makes the reader hold three clauses before the verb arrives. Split it: claim first, evidence second." |
| 2 | "Great job! A few issues with the methodology but overall really solid work. B+." | Inflating praise + unlocated problems | "Your design isolates the variable cleanly — that's the hard part, done. The gap: n=12 can't support the significance claim in §4. Next step: either temper the claim ('suggestive') or justify the sample. See me if you want the power-analysis shortcut." |
| 3 | "You need to work on your writing." | Self-adjacent, global, unactionable | "Two fixes will lift this paper most: (1) one claim per paragraph — §2's paragraphs each contain three; (2) topic sentences that state the claim, not the topic. Revise §2 with just these two rules before submitting the final." |
| 4 | "Did you even read the assignment prompt?" | Sarcasm; attacks the student, not the gap | "The prompt asks for a comparison of the two models; this submission analyzes only model A. The analysis itself is sound — for the regrade window, add §3 treating model B with the same structure you used for A." |
| 5 | "Good effort, but this isn't quite there yet. Keep working at it!" | Pure mood, zero information, effort-praise as consolation | "The proof strategy (induction on tree height) is the right one. It breaks at the inductive step: you assume the property for n+1 instead of proving it. Redo just that step — the rest stands." |
| 6 | "Perfect, no comments!" | Praise that can't be repeated; even strong work deserves one informative line | "Clean throughout. The strongest move: testing the boundary case before the general argument in Q3 — that habit is what made the rest short. Carry it into the final project." |

## Severity-consistency check (batch fairness)

Before the checkpoint, sort comments by rubric level and read within each level:
identical levels should receive comparable bluntness and comparable next-step demand.
A "developing"-level student who got gentle encouragement while another "developing"
student got a stern catalog is a fairness defect — surface it; the professor resolves it
(their notes may justify the difference; that's their call, made visibly).
