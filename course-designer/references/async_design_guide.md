# Async & Online Course Design Guide

Evidence base for `async_designer_agent`. Online/async is not in-person delivered through a
screen — synchrony, the room, and ambient presence are design defaults that disappear and
must be deliberately rebuilt. Agents cite this guide and Pedagogy Foundations §§7, 9 when
justifying a restructuring choice; artifacts stay citation-free.

## Community of Inquiry (CoI — Garrison, Anderson & Archer, 2000) {#coi}

Online learning that works sustains three interacting presences. Design each explicitly —
none is automatic once the room is gone:

- **Teaching presence** — visible design, facilitation, and direct instruction: the
  professor's voice in module intros, discussion synthesis, timely feedback, announcements.
  This is the presence most often lost in async and the one that most predicts satisfaction
  and persistence.
- **Social presence** — students projecting as real people: intro activities, peer
  interaction with names attached, small persistent groups, low-stakes community moves.
- **Cognitive presence** — the inquiry cycle (triggering question → exploration →
  integration → resolution). Async discussion protocols should drive toward integration,
  not stall at first-post-only.

## Chunking & cognitive load {#chunking}

Self-directed learners pace themselves; a 75-minute lecture recording is a wall.
- Segment content into short units (≈6–12 min video, one idea per segment — Mayer's
  segmenting principle; Pedagogy Foundations §9).
- Each module is **self-contained**: a learner can complete it without needing a live
  session to fill gaps. State objectives, give the input, give the practice, give the check.
- Front-load a module map so learners see the path and expected time before they start.

## Regular & substantive interaction (RSI) {#rsi}

The durable distinction (and, in the US, a regulatory one) between a real course and a
correspondence course: the instructor interacts **regularly** (predictable cadence) and
**substantively** (about the content — feedback, facilitation, synthesis — not just
auto-graded quizzes or a syllabus posted once). Design RSI in, don't assume it: scheduled
feedback windows, instructor presence in discussion, recorded module wrap-ups.

## Async engagement patterns {#engagement}

Synchronous interaction substitutes that work without a shared clock:

- **Structured discussion** — prompt → individual post by a date → required substantive
  peer responses by a later date → instructor synthesis. The synthesis is what makes it a
  discussion rather than parallel monologues; never skip it.
- **Roles & deadlines** — rotate roles (starter, synthesizer, devil's advocate) so peer
  interaction has structure; staggered deadlines force genuine response, not last-minute
  pile-on.
- **Small persistent groups** — a stable cohort over several modules builds social presence
  a whole-class forum cannot.
- **Asynchronous critique / peer review** — leans on the same evidence as TILT
  (Pedagogy Foundations §6): students need explicit criteria to give useful async feedback.
- **Presence touchpoints** — short instructor audio/video per module, prompt and visible
  feedback turnaround, a predictable weekly rhythm students can rely on.

## Sync vs async: what to keep live {#split}

Keep synchronous only what genuinely needs presence; push the rest async to respect
self-paced access (and time zones):
- **Stay sync (or sync-preferred):** live oral defenses and demos, real-time collaborative
  labs, high-bandwidth debate, first-week community building, exam-integrity-critical
  components.
- **Go async well:** lecture delivery, reading and drill, individual reflection, staged
  written work, anything a recording + structured follow-up serves as well or better.
- **Hybrid** keeps a deliberate, minimal sync core and makes everything else async — name
  the sync core's purpose, or it becomes attendance theater.

## Honest about transfer {#transfer}

What transfers from in-person: outcomes, assessment logic, the active-learning evidence
base (Pedagogy Foundations §4 — async polls, think-then-post). What does **not** transfer
cleanly: spontaneous discussion, ambient instructor presence, reading the room, peer energy.
Async substitutes for these (protocols, recorded presence, persistent groups) but does not
fully replace them — state the trade-off honestly to the professor rather than claiming
equivalence. Self-direction load is real: completion depends on structure and presence, not
on learner willpower.

## Accessibility is the floor, not a feature {#access}

Online removes the in-room cues some students relied on, so UDL (Pedagogy Foundations §7;
CAST UDL 3.0) is non-negotiable, baked into every module by default:
- Captions on all video; transcripts for all audio.
- Documents with real heading structure, readable contrast, descriptive link text, alt text.
- No information conveyed by color or sound alone.
- Multiple means of engagement and expression where the outcome allows it.
These help everyone in an async context, not only registered-accommodation students, and
they are defaults — not retrofits added after a complaint.
